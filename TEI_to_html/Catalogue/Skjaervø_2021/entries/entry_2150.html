Letter on the back of Chinese sutra Fo shuo guanding suiyuan wangsheng shi fang jing tu. The conservation paper now covers much of the top line as well as several line beginnings (as can been in the SD plate), but Bailey’s first reading antedates this. It is the end of P 2898 (KT II, p. 116 (62)). New edition in Kumamoto, 1991a.

translation:
l.2: ... on the [fifth] day you should go out (= leave).
l.3: And Cīka Gūlai deigned (send) an order to Dūṃ Saṃgalakā (?) that “As long as no order comes here
l.4: you can not go to the Land (= China).” I put a detailed report in the hand of Saṃgalakāṃ, but
l.5: no order came to me saying what I shall do. [25] He who is the jiedushi in Shazhou, he said: “To us here
l.6: no order has come at all regarding you, saying what you shall do. What are you doing here that you do not go away? [27] Even here in a foreign land
l.7: I have some stuff. And there are many envoys here, eastern ambassadors (ttūṅa-śīya = dongshi œ S) and others. When they ask us
l.8: “what are you (all) doing that you do not go away?” I do not know at all what you tell them(?).
l.9: Into whatever(?) villages in the land you go, the court ambassador (ttiṅa-śī = tingshi æ S) would come; and he brings us ... respect.
l.10: They do not at all take our men into account(?) {And those people with you then went, according to the royal order.} the way it may be proper for them. [29] When the order comes to us (telling us)
l.11: ... what we shall do, being in a *foreign land ... we shall die right away. [30] We then put the detailed
l.12: information and report to you in the hand of Majarūṣa (about) how there is only hardship for us here.
l.11a: If now the (humble) servant commits any faults toward the royal court, the men there will altogether daringly rush to his side. They will send no men to the side of humble servants.
