Pelliot chinois 4524

Section: r1
Illustrated transformation tableau (pien-hsiang 變相) scroll for use in transformation (pien 變) storytelling, i.e. storytelling with pictures. Unique and inestimably precious. Scenes on r° correspond exactly to those in the “Transformation Text on the Subjugation of Demons” 降魔變文 (T361–389), although the entire narrative does not survive on the present scroll. On v° of each scene are the verse portions (written in a mediocre hand with mistakes and corrections) of the transformation text which describe the episode depicted on r°. There are no prose passages on this scroll. Ordinary beige paper. The colored drawings exhibit a fairly high degree of technical skill and imagination. Especially to be noted is the artist’s deliberate employment of what I call the “continuous narrative” technique.


Section: v1
Difficult to decipher notation (upside-down in relation to verse on v° and pictures on r°): “We gave up on little Master So at his seat. Yüan-ch’ang sought for but couldn’t get this. Wen-pin took care of things.” 丟却索师兒坐上(?) [#86]昌覔(=覓)不得这(=這?) 文斌[#87](=料?)事. It is possible that this enigmatic notation refers to the means whereby the quondam owner(s) of this scroll came into possession of it. Yüan-ch’ang many be one of the inscribers of Pelliot chinois 2633. Wen-pin may be the official scribe who wrote Or.8210/S.2973 (dated the eight month of 970). The latter ms is a letter from the Chief Escort of the Military Governor and Overseer of Copyists Ma Wen-pin 節度押衙知司書手馬文斌 (so titled at head; cf. Pelliot chinois 3726v°). The letter and accompanying poem offer support for my surmise above (that Wen-pin was involved in the procurement of the illustrated transformation tableau scroll for storytelling now known as Pelliot chinois 4524). Both the letter and the poem clearly display Wen-pin’s intense attraction for vivid, dynamic Buddhist art:
Chief Escort of the Military Governor and Overseer of Copyists, Ma Wen-pin
I, the aforementioned Wen-pin, was accompanying your Honor while you stopped in this village when I saw a bejewelled elephant on a colourful wall-painting. Although I am not talented, I immediately composed a short poem made up of heptasyllabic verse with four rhymed couplets. I hereby respectfully present it to you with this letter, begging that you will favor it with you kind consideration when you read it. Respectfully I record the poem on this letter and present it to you in the form of previous respectful communications.
Letter of the Chief Escort of the Military Governor and Overseer of Copyists, Ma Wen-pin, on the …th day of the eighth month of the year 970.

The rare, bejewelled elephant is a king among beasts,
Throughout the world, there is no match for this fierce valor and heroic heart;
His four legs are planted as jade pillars,
His pair of tusks are sharp as the tip of a steel sword.
As he stands surveying the steep crags, he becomes a mountain,
When he moves, his swaying form terrifies the beholder;
Let his name alone be announced to evil types,
And they would not dare henceforth to create trouble.

One of the scenes on Pelliot chinois 4524r° depicts such an elephant as does Pelliot chinois 4518 (14) See also Pelliot chinois 6001 1. Very good hand, different from Pelliot chinois 4524v° (inscription). Some elements of the calligraphy are similar (e.g. 貟, 見) but Or.8210/S.2973 is much more deliberately and carefully written whereas Pelliot chinois 4524v° (inscription) is much larger, freer, and fainter. In Or.8210/S.2973, Wen-pin refers to himself in the first person, while on Pelliot chinois 4524v° (inscription), he is referred to in the third person. Or.8210/S.2973v° is a Buddhist text with dhāraṇī. Light beige paper.
